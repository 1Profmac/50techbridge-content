## The Morning After

Nobody talks about the morning after.

The morning after you make the big decision.
The morning after you walk away from the stable thing —
the salary, the title, the conference room with the
fluorescent lights and the agendas printed on paper.

The morning after you tell yourself — and anyone
who would listen — that you are building something new.

That morning has a particular quality.

It is quiet in a way that feels less like peace
and more like the absence of noise you had been
using to avoid hearing yourself think.

I have had that morning more than once in my life.

The morning after I decided to build my first
company — in the garage, before I knew what I
was doing, before I had proof that it would work.

The morning after I left Los Angeles and the
life I had built in the fashion industry.

And the morning after I decided to stop waiting
for the institution to become what it needed to be —
and to go build that thing myself.

Each time, the morning was the same.

The vision was still there. The decision was still right.
And the fear was enormous.

## The Voice

You know the voice.

If you are 50-plus and you have ever tried to change
direction — start a business, learn a new skill,
pivot a career, walk away from something that wasn't
working anymore — you know the voice.

It is not loud. That is the thing about it.
It doesn't announce itself.

It just narrates.

*You're too old for this.*
*Other people know how to do this. You don't.*
*You had your chance. You should have done this years ago.*
*What makes you think you're qualified?*
*Who do you think you are?*

The voice is not random. It didn't appear from nowhere.
It was built over decades — assembled from
the things people said and didn't say, from systems
that excluded and overlooked and dismissed,
from a childhood that had more violence in it
than any child should carry, from a world that handed
me an ADD diagnosis at 20 and a label where
a map should have been.

I have been hearing that voice my whole life.

What changed — what graduate school at USM gave me
the tools to understand — was not how to silence it.

It was how to answer it.

---

## Ron and Mary Hulnick

In the 1990s, I sat in a classroom at the University
of Santa Monica and heard something that
reorganized the way I understood my own life.

Drs. Ron and Mary Hulnick — the founders of USM,
the architects of what they call Soul-Centered Education —
have spent more than four decades teaching a
deceptively simple idea:

*The wound is where the light enters.*

Not the wound as obstacle.
Not the wound as damage to be managed or overcome
or pushed past on the way to something better.

The wound as the specific place — the exact location —
where something real and necessary enters your life.

I had been carrying my wounds for a long time
before I understood them this way.

The early childhood. The violence. The poverty.
The ADD brain that school didn't know what to do with.
The decades of working twice as hard to be taken
half as seriously. The invisibility — first as a
Black man in rooms that didn't expect me,
then as a man over 50 in a world that had
quietly decided my best days were behind me.

I had been treating those wounds as the explanation
for why things were hard.

USM — Ron and Mary — offered a different frame.

What if the wounds were not the reason for the struggle?
What if they were the reason for the work?

What if everything that had been hard was exactly
the preparation for everything that needed to be done?

---

## Compassionate Self-Forgiveness

The practice that changed my life has a formal name
at USM: Compassionate Self-Forgiveness.

It sounds soft. I want to be clear that it is not.

It is one of the harder things I have done.

The structure is simple. You name the judgment
you are holding against yourself — the verdict
you have accepted about who you are or what
you deserve — and you release it.

Not because the painful thing didn't happen.
Not because the judgment was entirely wrong.
But because carrying it forward costs more
than it teaches.

*"I forgive myself for judging myself as someone
who started too late."*

*"I forgive myself for believing that my ADD
brain was a limitation rather than a different
kind of strength."*

*"I forgive myself for the years I spent
waiting for permission from people who were
never going to give it."*

*"I forgive myself for judging myself as less than —
for accepting, in small ways, the verdict
that the world kept handing me."*

Each one of those forgiveness statements
is a door.

When you walk through it, you don't find
a version of yourself that never got hurt.

You find a version of yourself that is no longer
organized around the hurt.

That version can build things.

But forgiveness alone is not a destination.
It is a clearing.

What you do with the cleared space is the next question.
And that is where the second great teaching from
Ron and Mary comes in.

## Intention — Setting It and Keeping It

Ron and Mary Hulnick returned to intention
constantly throughout the Masters program.

Not as a productivity concept. Not as goal-setting.
As a spiritual practice.

The difference matters.

Setting a goal is an intellectual exercise.
You identify what you want, you write it down,
you build a plan. The goal lives in your head.

Setting an intention is something deeper.

An intention lives in your body.
It is a commitment made not just to what you want
to accomplish but to who you are committed to being
while you accomplish it.

Ron and Mary would ask:

*What is your intention for this?*

Not what do you want to achieve.
What are you intending — in your character,
in your conduct, in how you will treat the people
around you, in the quality of presence you will
bring to the work?

And then — this is the harder part:

*Are you keeping it?*

Not periodically checking in. Not reviewing your
quarterly goals. Keeping it.

In the room. In the conversation.
In the moment when things get hard and
the easy path is to cut a corner or
dismiss someone's concern or
let the momentum of busyness
carry you past the thing that actually matters.

*Are you keeping your intention right now?*

I have asked myself that question ten thousand times
in the building of 50+TechBridge.

When I was exhausted and the grant application
was due and the training session needed prep
and the community needed attention and the
technology wasn't working — am I keeping my intention?

The intention was never to build a successful business.

The intention was to show up for a generation
that had been waiting for someone to show up for them.

Those are not the same thing.
The first one can be faked. The second one cannot.

When I kept the intention — when I came back to it
in the hard moments, the way Ron and Mary taught —
the work had a quality it didn't have otherwise.

People felt it.

That is not mystical. It is practical.
When you are doing something because you genuinely
intend it — not for the outcome, not for the
validation, but because you are committed to the
person you are being in service of something that
matters — the work is different.

And people can tell.

## What the Wounds Were For

I want to tell you what I understand now
that I didn't understand in that garage in Los Angeles,
or in those conference rooms, or in the long years
of carrying the weight of early trauma and
late diagnosis and institutional exclusion.

Every wound has a corresponding gift.

Not because pain is good. Not because suffering
builds character in some simple motivational-poster way.

Because the specific texture of a wound —
what it cost you, what it taught you to notice,
what it made you determined to change —
is often the precise shape of the contribution
you are most equipped to make.

I grew up in circumstances that taught me to read
a room, to find the exits, to move before things
got worse. That is not a pleasant origin story.
But it is also, directly, the skill that let me
see what was coming in the garment industry before
my competitors did, and what was failing at the
institution I worked for before the data confirmed it.

I was diagnosed with ADD at 20 and spent years
believing it meant I couldn't focus.
What it actually meant was that my brain is
wired for connection-making — for seeing patterns
across domains that specialists miss because
they are too deep in their lane to look sideways.
That is not a deficit in a world that needs people
who can build bridges between the old and the new.

I felt invisible — as a Black man, as a 50-plus person,
as someone the credential system had never fully
claimed — and that invisibility taught me exactly
what it feels like to be overlooked.

Which means I know, specifically and personally,
what it costs.

Which means when I fight for the community
of adults 50 and over who are being systematically
excluded from the digital future —
I am not fighting from theory.

I am fighting from the wound.

And Ron and Mary Hulnick taught me that
the wound is exactly where the light gets in.

## The Gift of the Hard Childhood

I do not romanticize where I came from.

Early trauma is not a prerequisite for success.
Violence is not character-building. Poverty is not
a gift that teaches you hustle. These are things
that happen to children who did not choose them
and do not deserve them — and the world should
do better by those children.

I say this clearly because I have seen too many
versions of the "I came from nothing and now look"
story weaponized against the very communities
the storyteller came from. As if the lesson is
that the hard circumstances were the point.

They were not the point.

The point is what you do with what you carry.

What I carried was this:
I learned, very young, that the world was not going
to show up for me automatically. That waiting for
the system to fix itself was a strategy for
permanent disappointment. That the only reliable
option was to keep my head down, keep moving,
and build what was needed whether or not I
had permission.

I worked 16-hour days in Los Angeles not because
I was grinding for a lifestyle.

I worked 16-hour days because that's what
keeping moving looked like.

And when I sat down to build 50+TechBridge —
when the institution hadn't worked and the system
hadn't worked and the conventional path had run out —
I already knew how to keep moving.

The wound had taught me.

## To You

I am telling you all of this because this book
is not only about technology.

It is about what happens when a person who has been
written off — by circumstance, by diagnosis, by age,
by the quiet daily machinery of exclusion —
decides to rewrite the story.

That rewriting is not a single moment.
It is a practice.

It involves the tools in the chapters that follow —
the AI and the automation and the platforms and
the skills that can rebuild a career or launch
a business or connect you to a community that
sees you clearly.

But it also involves this:

Forgiving yourself for the time it took.
Forgiving yourself for the years of waiting.
Forgiving yourself for believing, even briefly,
that the verdict was final.

Ron and Mary Hulnick taught me that the wound
is not the end of the story.

It is the beginning of the light.

And if you are 50-plus — if you have been carrying
the weight of a story that says your time is over,
your best years are behind you, the window has closed —

I want you to try something.

Right now. Before you turn the page.

Say it out loud or say it silently.
Say it to yourself the way you would say it
to someone you love.

*"I forgive myself for believing I was done."*

Then — once you've said it and meant it —
do the second thing.

Set your intention.

Not your goal. Not your plan.
Your intention.

Ask yourself:

*Who am I committed to being as I build this next chapter?*

Write it down if you can. One sentence.
Something you can return to when the work gets hard —
and it will get hard — and ask yourself:
am I keeping this?

That is the practice Ron and Mary taught.

Forgiveness clears the ground.
Intention builds on it.

Those two things together are more powerful
than any technology in this book.

And this book has a lot of technology in it.
